Random Seed

 

Concept

Once, owning a piece of vinyl was the only way to have a copy of a song. In that sense, the vinyl was the single or album. Now you could listen to a piece of music without there being any associated object. Music no longer has any intrinsic physical form. It could be on YouTube, on a CD, or just an MP3 file.

The question that started the Random Seed project was: How could a composition be inherently physical?

I've been working with composer Tom Bryan to explore different compositional methods that answer this question.

It's a tough problem, because no matter what you do to protect a piece of music to prevent it being digitised, someone can record with a microphone it and upload it to YouTube.

Unless. Unless it's different each time. A device that plays back a song which is (slightly) different each time can't be uploaded anywhere. That's what Random Seed intends to be.

An interesting consequence of this is that Random Seed takes full advantage of what the web can do - go beyond linear playback, taking full advantage of the technology.

The 3 minute pop song, the 11 track album or the arrangement of choral music have all been influenced by the way they are performed - why not music which is played back by computer?

 

Development

Below are two experimental versions of Random Seed, highlight our creative journey so far. Please be kind to them, they are prototypes.

You'll need to let most of the samples load completely before pressing play. Pressing the randomise button will give you a new version of the music, you'll have to rewind and press play again to hear it.

 

Memba Dat

Memba Dat is an ambient piece, where all of the samples are composed to work together in many different combinations.

 

Radiohead - Reckoner

This is a reworking of the Radiohead single Reckoner. They released stems for the song as part of a remix competition. This piece explores composing with a random element while using conventionally structured music.
